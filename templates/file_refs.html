BIA File References

Every BLM allotment patent that passed through the Bureau of Indian Affairs in the early twentieth century carries a small string of digits referring to a folder of correspondence at the National Archives. This page indexes those references and reveals the administrative episodes behind allotment.

What these files are

From 1907 to 1975 the Bureau of Indian Affairs maintained a single master correspondence archive called the Central Classified Files (CCF). Every administrative matter touching Indian affairs — allotments, fee patent conversions, school enrollments, tribal council decisions, leases, individual rights, complaints, cancellations — flowed through this system. Each file was assigned a number in the form NNNNN-YY (a sequential letter number plus the two-digit year the file was opened) and a decimal subject classification (e.g. 312 for Allotments, 304 for Tribal Affairs). A complete CCF citation reads letter number / year / decimal classification / agency or jurisdiction.

When the BLM issued an allotment patent, the clerk often stamped the relevant BIA file number onto the patent — sometimes labeled "I.O." (Indian Office), sometimes unlabeled. The CCF number is, in effect, a pointer to the paper record of how that patent came to exist: the correspondence between BIA agents, superintendents, the Commissioner of Indian Affairs, and (sometimes) the allottees themselves. Because BIA processed allotments in batches under shared files, the same CCF number appears on many patents — clustering by reference exposes the administrative episodes (sometimes thousands of patents wide) that produced them.

For the full archival context — the three NARA-numbered series (121A 1907–1939; 121B 1940–1957; 121C 1958–1975), the digitized index card collection through 1942, and the structure of the decimal classification scheme — see the National Archives' Central Classified Files research guide.
