About the Murray Memorandum

In 1958, Senator James E. Murray of Montana published Indian Land Transactions through the Senate Committee on Interior and Insular Affairs. The nearly 900-page report documented how individual Indian trust land was disposed of during the decade following World War II — the height of the federal termination era.

During this period (1947–1957), Congress pursued the termination of federal trust responsibilities for Indian tribes. House Concurrent Resolution 108 (1953) declared it congressional policy to end federal supervision over selected tribes "at the earliest possible time."

The Murray data shows that individual Indian trust land fell from 15.9 million acres in 1947 to 12.6 million in 1957 — a net loss of 3.3 million acres in a single decade. Meanwhile, tribal trust land actually increased from 33.6 million to 39.9 million acres, partly through federal land acquisitions.

The memorandum records 18,546 trust removal transactions across 52 BIA agencies, with the largest losses concentrated at agencies in Montana, the Dakotas, and the Pacific Northwest — many of the same reservations that had experienced the heaviest forced fee patenting a generation earlier.
