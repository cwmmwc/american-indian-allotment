Alienation Rate vs. Forced Fee Claims

Two related but distinct measures. Wilson's "alienation rate" tracks land that left Indian ownership entirely (sold to non-Indians) by 1934. The FR forced fee claims are the ~9,500 formal claims filed in the 1983 Federal Register by allottees or heirs who believed their trust land was converted to fee status without consent. These claims represent a small fraction of all trust-to-fee conversions — most conversions did not result in a formal claim. Where the red bar exceeds zero but the alienation bar is low (as with Klamath), it suggests trust-to-fee conversion happened but the land had not yet been sold by 1934.

About This Data

The Wilson Report (1935) was compiled by the Bureau of Indian Affairs to document the condition of Indian reservations as of June 30, 1934. Table VI provides per-reservation data on original areas, allotments made, land alienated through sales and fee patents, and remaining tribal holdings. This data is cross-referenced with BLM General Land Office patent records and Federal Register forced fee claims (1983) to show how allotment-era land loss connects to later claims for redress.

The Wilson Report records 23.2 million acres alienated across all reservations. Of that, 14.2 million (61%) was from the Five Civilized Tribes (Cherokee, Choctaw, Chickasaw, Creek, Seminole), whose allotments in Indian Territory were administered under the Curtis Act and Dawes Commission — a separate system not covered by BLM General Land Office patent records. Osage accounts for another 1 million acres. The charts comparing Wilson data with BLM patents cover 93 of 212 reservations (7.7M alienated acres), representing the reservations allotted under the general allotment system.

Reservation names were mapped between datasets using manual matching. Unmatched reservations include the Five Civilized Tribes, Osage, Pueblos (communal land grants), Mission rancherias, and New York state reservations.

The Murray Memorandum (1958) documented trust land transactions during 1947–1957, the termination era. It records individual and tribal land holdings at the start and end of the decade, trust removal transactions, and lands taken for public purposes. Murray agencies are BIA jurisdictions, often covering multiple tribes. Mapping to BLM preferred names covers 35 of 52 agencies. Together, Wilson (1934) and Murray (1947–1957) bracket the allotment and termination eras, showing two distinct waves of Indian land loss. See the full Murray Memorandum page.
