About This Data

The Federal Register Notices

On March 31, 1983, and again on November 7, 1983, the Bureau of Indian Affairs published notices in the Federal Register listing Indian allotment claims that had been filed with the government. These notices were part of the BIA's attempt to inventory allotments where fee patents had been issued without the allottee's consent — so-called "forced fee patents."

The notices listed 35,686 claims organized by BIA agency code, identifying the allottee, allotment number, and case number for each claim. Of these, 9,649 are forced fee patent claims. The remainder include trespass, tax forfeiture, secretarial transfers, unapproved land sales, and dozens of other claim types.

What Is a "Forced Fee Patent"?

Under the General Allotment Act of 1887 (Dawes Act), tribal lands were divided into individual allotments held in trust by the federal government for 25 years. During the trust period, the land could not be sold or taxed.

A fee patent converted an allotment from trust status to fee simple ownership, making it alienable (sellable) and taxable. A forced fee patent was one issued without the allottee's consent, removing federal trust protections whether or not the allottee wanted or understood the change.

The Burke Act of 1906 gave the Secretary of the Interior authority to issue fee patents to "competent" allottees before the trust period expired. Beginning in 1907 and accelerating dramatically after Commissioner Cato Sells' 1917 "Declaration of Policy," the government issued thousands of forced fee patents, especially to allottees determined to be of "less than half Indian blood."

The consequences were devastating. Once a fee patent was issued, the allottee owed property taxes — often with no understanding that the change had occurred. Within a few years, many allottees lost their land to tax sales, fraud, or sales under economic duress. By some estimates, forced fee patenting caused the loss of millions of acres of Indian land.

What This Dataset Shows — and What It Doesn't

The 9,649 forced fee claims in this dataset represent a floor, not a ceiling. The allottees or their dependents who appear in this dataset are those who:

  • knew about the Federal Register notice,
  • understood the claims process,
  • had documentation of the forced fee patent,
  • filed their claim before the deadline

The actual number of forced fee patents is certainly higher.

Claim-to-Patent Linkage

Of the 35,686 Federal Register claims, 7,225 have been hand-verified and linked to at least one BLM patent record in the General Land Office database. The remaining 28,461 claims exist in the Federal Register but have not been matched to a patent record. On individual claim pages, a fallback search by allotment number and tribe sometimes locates a likely patent match, but these have not been verified.

An additional 130 tribes and agency groupings with fewer than 100 claims account for about 3,300 more unlinked claims — most of these have zero linkages. In total, 181 tribes have no verified patent linkages at all, representing 10,380 claims.

Why the gap exists: The linkage process required manually matching each Federal Register claim to a BLM patent record by name, allotment number, and tribe — a labor-intensive process done through a Rails administrative interface. Some tribes (Round Valley, Klamath, Cheyenne-Arapaho, Yankton Sioux) were nearly fully processed. Others — including large groups like Fort Peck, Turtle Mountain Chippewa, Cherokee, and the California Indians — were never started. The partially linked tribes (Cheyenne River, Rosebud, Blackfeet, Pine Ridge) reflect work that was begun but not completed.

Even when a patent exists for a given claim, automatic matching is complicated by a naming mismatch between the Federal Register and the BLM's General Land Office records. The Federal Register organized claims by BIA administrative agency — so all claims processed through the White Earth agency are labeled "White Earth Chippewa," regardless of the allottee's original reservation. BLM patent records use the original allotting reservation — "Fond du Lac Chippewa," "Leech Lake Chippewa," etc. The same person's claim and patent can carry different tribe names, making automated linkage by allotment number and tribe unreliable across agency boundaries. Individual claim and patent pages note this issue and link to cross-searches by allotment number.

The "Patent Date" column on the claims search page shows "No match" for claims that lack a verified linkage. Individual claim pages may still display a probable patent match found by allotment number and tribe, but these automatic matches have not been hand-verified.

Download: Unlinked claims with fallback matches (CSV) — all 28,461 unlinked claims with the best available fee patent match by allotment number and tribe. Of these, 5,271 matched at least one fee patent; 23,189 had no match. Claims with match_count > 1 may be ambiguous.

BIA Agency Codes

The Federal Register notices organized claims by the Bureau of Indian Affairs' administrative hierarchy. Each claim carries an agency code — an alphanumeric identifier that encodes the BIA area office and local agency responsible for the allotment. For example, A00007 identifies the Flandreau agency under the Aberdeen area office, while C51201 identifies the Blackfeet agency under the Billings area office.

Policy Eras Visible in the Data

The distribution of forced fee patents by year reflects the changing federal policies toward Indian land:

1887 — General Allotment Act (Dawes Act)
Authorized allotment of tribal lands to individual Indians. Established the 25-year trust period.
1902 — Dead Indian Land Act
Allowed sale of inherited allotments, accelerating land transfer out of Indian ownership.
1906 — Burke Act
Gave the Secretary of the Interior power to declare allottees "competent" and issue fee patents before the trust period expired.
1917 — Declaration of Policy
Commissioner Cato Sells began issuing forced fee patents under the Burke Act authority. The first wave of forced patenting.
1917–1920 — Sells Declaration and Competency Commissions
Commissioner Cato Sells declared all Indians of "less than half Indian blood" to be competent, issuing fee patents en masse. Competency commissions traveled to reservations, often issuing patents after cursory or no individual assessment.
1920 — Order of Restoration
At the end of 1920, Secretary of the Interior John Barton Payne abandoned the Declaration of Policy, though by then the damage was largely done.
1934 — Indian Reorganization Act
Ended the allotment policy and attempted to restore tribal self-governance and land holdings.

Data Coverage: Patents and the Map

The research database contains 285,870 allotment patents from the Bureau of Land Management General Land Office. Of these, 239,845 are displayed on the allotment map — matched to parcels in the Public Land Survey System (PLSS). The remaining 46,025 patents are searchable on the patents page but cannot be shown on the map.

The gap exists because the map was built by joining patent records to PLSS polygon geometry. Patents that could not be matched to a physical parcel were excluded. The largest gaps are:

CategoryMissingReason
Trust patents (AZ/NM)~12,000Navajo, Gila River, and Colorado River reservations use non-rectangular (metes-and-bounds) surveys outside the PLSS grid
Serial Land Patents~27,000Patents with legal descriptions that did not match PLSS parcels — incomplete or non-standard township/range/section data
State Land Patents~14,000Many in Alabama and Mississippi (Chickasaw/Choctaw removal-era), where tribal land surveys predate the rectangular system
Other types~4,000Indian Allotment, Sioux Scrip, Chippewa Treaty, and miscellaneous document types

Key implication: Arizona and New Mexico reservations are significantly underrepresented on the map. Navajo allotments in particular are almost entirely absent because the reservation's survey system is incompatible with PLSS. Similarly, southeastern removal-era patents (Alabama, Mississippi) are largely missing. Any analysis of national allotment patterns should account for this southwestern and southeastern gap.

The patents page can be filtered to show only the 46,025 non-mappable patents, or use the "Map Status" dropdown to see both together.

Data Sources

  • Federal Register claims: Digitized from the March 31, 1983 and November 7, 1983 Federal Register notices
  • BLM patent records: Bureau of Land Management General Land Office Records, accessed via glorecords.blm.gov
  • Patent-to-claim linkages: Hand-verified matching between Federal Register claims and GLO patent records
