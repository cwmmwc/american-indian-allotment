Trust → Fee Linkages

Pairings between the two patents that documented a single allotment's passage from federal trust to private fee ownership — the legal mechanism through which the great majority of allotment land left Indian hands.

What these linkages show

Under the General Allotment Act of 1887 (the Dawes Act) and its successors, reservation land was divided into parcels and assigned to individual Indian allottees. Each allotment was first issued as a trust patent: the federal government held legal title for an initial period of twenty-five years (often administratively extended), during which the land could not be sold, mortgaged, taxed, or otherwise alienated. When the trust period ended — by expiration, by application, or, after 1917, often by the BIA's unilateral decision under the "forced fee" policy — the trust patent was replaced by a fee patent. With the fee patent the allottee acquired full legal title; the land also became taxable and salable. Most allotments were lost to tax foreclosure, sale, or fraud within a few years of fee conversion.

In the BLM catalog the trust patent and the fee patent appear as two separate records, identical in their land description but issued years (often decades) apart under different authorities. Each linkage on this page pairs the trust patent for an allotment with the fee patent that succeeded it. With the pairing, every allotment becomes a story you can trace: who received it, when it was placed in trust, how long it remained so, and when (and under what authority) it was converted out. The median gap from trust to fee across the recovered corpus is {% if totals.median_gap_years is not none %}{{ totals.median_gap_years|round(0, 'floor')|int }} years{% else %}—{% endif %} — long enough that the patentee's surname has often changed (marriage, remarriage), which is itself a useful corroboration signal.

Without these linkages the BLM patent catalog reads as two disconnected piles of records. With them, the catalog becomes a longitudinal source for land-dispossession research — countable, mappable, and queryable by tribe, agency, year, or PLSS parcel. This page is distinct from, and supplements, the older trust_fee_linkages table that was built by matching allotment numbers.

How these linkages were recovered (technical)

The catalog itself does not store the trust→fee pairing as a structured field. We recovered it from two independent passes:

  1. Remarks parsing (remarks_regex_v2). When BLM transcribed allotment patents into the modern catalog, staff often typed pointers into the remarks field like SEE SERIAL PATENT NR 75921-09 FOR FEE PATENT on trust patents whose conversion had been recorded. A regex pass extracts every accession-shaped token from those clauses (the v2 regex captures sibling references in NR X AND Y FOR FEE PATENT patterns that an earlier version dropped on the floor), and a validator confirms each extracted accession against the patent catalog by exact match, normalized match (stripping leading zeros / formatting variation), or short edit-distance fuzzy match (capped at distance 2 — typically single-digit transcription typos).
  2. Parcel + name matching (parcel_match_v1). Some trust patents have a typo in the remarks reference; others have no remarks at all. For these we match trusts to fees on shared PLSS coordinates (state + county + township + range + section + aliquot subdivision) plus at least one shared allottee name token — the second condition is what catches Lizzie Dowd's 1891 trust patent linking to a 1906 fee patent issued to "Lizzie D. Gilman; Lizzie Dowd" (marriage between trust and fee).

Both layers write to the same trust_fee_linkages_recovered table with a source column distinguishing them, and a UNIQUE (trust_accession, fee_accession) constraint that de-duplicates any pair both methods discover. A CHECK (trust_accession <> fee_accession) constraint prevents self-references at the database level.

See the project notes and the README for the bug history that produced the v2 regex and the parcel layer.
